Central Theme

Recap:

  • established that a sample statistic can be used to estimate an unknown population quantity
  • estimator : defines how the data are used to approximate the population quantity [i.e.: mean, sample proportion, regression coefficient]

Current Aim: Evaluate the quality of the estimator

Indicators of a good estimator:

  1. target the correct population quantity
  2. vary as little as possible across repeated samples

Estimator checks:

  • determine whether the estimator is centered on the target parameter

  • observe how widely the estimator varies across repeated samples

  • determine whether the design assumptions needed for that estimator are plausible

  • here the sampling distribution is a key tool for judging estimator quality

Point Estimation

  • point estimate : single numerical value computed from sample data to approximate an unknown population parameter

  • interpretable quantities derived from data [i.e.: average treatment effects, disease prevalence, or risk differences] and serve as the basis for confidence intervals, hypothesis tests

Two examples include:

Sample Mean

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \]

and

Sample Proportion

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} \]

  • \(\bar{x}\): sample mean \(\rightarrow\) estimate of a population mean \(\mu\)
  • \(x_i\): the value for the \(i\)th sampled observation
  • \(n\): sample size
  • \(\hat{p}\): sample proportion \(\rightarrow\) estimate of a population proportion \(p\)
  • \(x\): number of observations in the sample with the characteristic of interest

Estimation in Biomedical Inference

  • estimation is used to quantify key quantities such as average biomarker level, disease prevalence, treatment response, or average processing time

  • biased estimator can systematically misstate the underlying biology or clinical burden

  • highly variable estimator can produce unstable results that differ substantially from one study sample to another

Common Failures in Statistical Estimation

estimator can fail [provide a poor estimation for the true population parameter] in two ways:

  • exhibiting systematic deviation from the target bias

  • displaying excessive sampling variability [high variance across repeated samples

  • estimator can fail because it is biased: estimator systematically misses the target

  • estimator lacks accuracy if it does not target the true parameter on average bias

  • estimator can be too variable: if estimator is centered correctly after multiple samples each individual sample-based estimates move around too much

  • estimator lacks precision if it is too variable across samples [variance]

The three panels therefore represent three distinct cases:

  • biased estimator: sampling distribution is shifted away from the true value [systematic error]
  • unbiased imprecise estimator: centered at the true value but with large spread [high variability]
  • unbiased and precise estimator: centered at the true value with small spread [low variability]

good estimation requires both correct centering [low bias] and tight concentration [low variance]

Bias

Bias measures systematic deviation from the true parameter:

\[ \operatorname{Bias}(\hat{\theta}) = E(\hat{\theta}) - \theta \]

Where:

  • \(\hat{\theta}\): estimator

  • \(E(\hat{\theta})\): expected value or average of the estimator across repeated samples

  • \(\theta\): true population parameter being estimated

  • bias = zero : estimator is unbiased

  • bias = negative/positive value : average falls above or below the true parameter \(\rightarrow\) estimator is biased

Unbiased and Biased Estimators

  • unbiased statistic :
    • sampling distribution centered at the true parameter
    • over many repeated samples estimate does not overestimate or underestimate the target
  • biased statistic :
    • estimator whose sampling distribution is centered away from the true parameter
      • sampling distribution centered away from the target

Precision and Variance

  • precision : describes how tightly an estimator clusters
  • estimator variance: expected squared distance between the estimator and the repeated-sample mean

Variance Formula

\[ \operatorname{Var}(\hat{\theta}) = E\left[(\hat{\theta} - E(\hat{\theta}))^2\right] \]

  • \(\operatorname{Var}(\hat{\theta})\): variance of the estimator

  • \(\hat{\theta}\): random variable [estimator]

  • \(E(\hat{\theta})\): center of the estimator across multiple samples

  • smaller variance: indicates that the estimator is more tightly concentrated around its expected value across repeated samples

  • larger variance : indicates that the estimator is more dispersed across repeated samples

Accuracy Versus Precision

accuracy and precision: statistical properties that describe how an estimator’s sampling distribution relates to the true parameter value in terms of its center and spread

  • accuracy bias : metric to describe how close the center of the estimator’s sampling distribution is to the true parameter value

Example: accurate estimator is centered at the target in repeated sampling

  • precision [variability]: metric describing the spread of the estimator’s sampling distribution

Example: precise estimator has less sampling variability \(\rightarrow\) repeated samples produce estimates that are more tightly clustered around the center of the distribution

Research Question Background: Chronic Kidney Disease

  • chronic kidney disease (CKD): sustained reduction in kidney function and is a major contributor to morbidity and mortality at the population level
  • United States approximately 14–15% of adults are estimated to have CKD
  • kidney function is quantified using estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) - continuous measure derived from serum creatinine and patient characteristics that approximates filtration capacity

estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR]

  • ≥ 90 → generally considered normal kidney function
  • 60–89 → mildly reduced kidney function
  • < 60 → clinically meaningful reduction, consistent with chronic kidney disease (CKD)

Summary

  • useful estimators generally have two properties:

    • low bias [accuracy]

      • estimator is centered near the true population parameter
      • example: repeated mean ≈ 61.4 with negligible bias

    • low variance [precision]

      • estimates vary minimally across repeated samples

  • bias can arise from systematic issues in data collection or sampling design

    • precision improves with larger sample size
